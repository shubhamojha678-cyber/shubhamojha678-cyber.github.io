Default Bail Under Section 187 BNSS: The 60/90-Day Rule Explained

By Shubham Ojha, BA LL.B., LL.M., ICWA (Foundation) | Advocate, Rajasthan High Court, Jodhpur
April 2026·9 min read

If someone you know has been arrested and is languishing in judicial custody while the police investigation drags on, the procedure for seeking their release can feel overwhelming. There are bail applications, anticipatory bail provisions, and a thicket of procedural requirements. But buried within the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS) — the statute that replaced the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 — is a provision that many accused persons and even some lawyers overlook. It is called default bail, also known as statutory bail or compulsive bail. And it is, in the right circumstances, an indefeasible right — one that the court must grant if the conditions are met.

Let us walk through it step by step.

The basic principle: investigation cannot be open-ended

When a person is arrested for a cognisable offence, the police are expected to complete their investigation and file a chargesheet (now called a police report under the BNSS) within a specified period. The law recognises that it would be fundamentally unjust to keep an accused person in custody indefinitely while the State takes its time building a case. So the legislature fixed outer time limits. If the investigating agency fails to file the police report within those limits, the accused acquires a right to be released on bail — not because the court has evaluated the merits of the case, but simply because the State has failed to complete its task in time.

This is the essence of default bail under Section 187 of the BNSS (formerly Section 167 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973).

The 60-day and 90-day periods

The time limits are straightforward:

  • 90 days — where the offence under investigation is punishable with death, life imprisonment, or imprisonment for a term of not less than ten years.
  • 60 days — for all other offences.

These periods are counted from the date the accused was first remanded to judicial custody — not from the date of arrest, and not from the date the FIR was registered. The distinction matters. An accused may spend a day or two in police custody before being produced before the Magistrate and remanded to judicial custody. The clock for default bail begins on that first remand.

So if a person is arrested for an offence punishable with, say, seven years' imprisonment — an offence under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS) that does not attract the 90-day window — and the police have not filed their report within 60 days of the first judicial remand, the accused has a right to walk out on bail. If the offence is punishable with ten years or more, the window extends to 90 days. And that is all there is to the arithmetic.

Why it is called an "indefeasible" right

The Supreme Court has been emphatic — and consistent — on this point. Default bail is not discretionary bail. The Magistrate is not weighing the gravity of the offence, the likelihood of the accused fleeing, or the strength of the prosecution's case. Once the statutory period has expired without a police report being filed, and the accused applies for bail, the Magistrate is bound to release them.

In Sanjay Dutt v. State through CBI (1994), a Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court held that the right to default bail is an indefeasible right that accrues when the chargesheet is not filed within the prescribed period. The court drew a sharp line: this right flows from the legislative mandate itself and cannot be defeated by the prosecution's plea that the investigation is complex or that further time is needed. The judgment remains the bedrock authority on the subject, and courts across India — including the Rajasthan High Court at both the Jodhpur and Jaipur benches — routinely rely on it.

In Uday Mohanlal Acharya v. State of Maharashtra (2001), the Supreme Court reinforced the position, holding that once the right to default bail has accrued, it cannot be taken away even if the chargesheet is subsequently filed — provided the accused had already applied for bail before the filing.

The critical condition: you must apply

And here is where many accused persons — and, regrettably, their lawyers — stumble. The right to default bail does not operate automatically. The accused must apply for it. The right accrues the moment the statutory period expires without a police report, but it must be exercised by filing an application before the Magistrate. If the accused does not apply, and the prosecution files the chargesheet even one day later, the window closes.

This point cannot be overstressed. In our practice in Jodhpur, we have seen cases where the accused was entitled to default bail but did not apply in time — sometimes because they were unaware of the provision, sometimes because their advocate was tracking a different timeline. By the time an application was moved, the chargesheet had already been filed, and the right stood extinguished.

The sequence, then, is everything:

  • Day 1: First remand to judicial custody.
  • Day 60 or Day 90 (as the case may be): Statutory period expires. If no police report has been filed, the right accrues.
  • The accused files an application for default bail before the police report is filed.
  • The Magistrate grants bail — this is mandatory, not discretionary.

If the chargesheet is filed before the application is made — even if the statutory period had already expired — the accused loses the right. The Supreme Court in Sanjay Dutt itself clarified this. The right is indefeasible once exercised; but it can be defeated by the filing of the chargesheet if the accused has been sitting on their hands.

What counts as filing of the police report?

A question that frequently arises in practice is what constitutes a valid filing. The police report must be filed before the Magistrate — not merely dispatched from the police station or handed to the court's filing counter after hours. Courts have held that the report must be presented before the Magistrate and taken on record. An incomplete chargesheet, or one that is filed without the essential documents and statements under Section 193 of the BNSS (formerly Section 173 CrPC), may not qualify as a valid police report for the purposes of defeating the right to default bail.

This has given rise to considerable litigation. The prosecution sometimes files what is effectively a skeleton chargesheet — bare-bones, without supporting material — simply to defeat the default bail application. Courts have taken a dim view of this. If the chargesheet is so deficient that it does not comply with the statutory requirements, it may not be treated as a valid filing, and the right to default bail would survive.

Multiple offences: which period applies?

Cases are rarely simple. An accused may be charged with several offences, some attracting the 60-day window and others the 90-day window. Which period governs? The answer is that the longest applicable period controls. If even one of the offences under investigation is punishable with death, life imprisonment, or ten or more years, the 90-day period applies to the entire investigation. The accused cannot claim default bail at 60 days for the lesser offences while remaining in custody for the graver ones — the periods are not severable in that way.

But there is a subtlety. If the police report is filed for some offences within the statutory period but the investigation into other offences continues, the position becomes more complex. The Supreme Court has held that where a chargesheet is filed for certain offences and the accused is in custody only in respect of those offences, default bail does not arise for those charges. But for offences where no chargesheet has been filed within the prescribed period, the right may still accrue. These situations require careful, case-specific analysis.

Can conditions be imposed?

Yes. Default bail is a right, but it is still bail — and the court may impose reasonable conditions. The Magistrate may require the accused to furnish a personal bond with sureties, surrender their passport, mark attendance at the police station, or refrain from contacting witnesses. What the Magistrate cannot do is refuse bail altogether on merits. The distinction is between refusing bail (impermissible) and imposing conditions on the grant of bail (permissible).

In practice, district courts in Rajasthan typically impose conditions that are broadly in line with what one would expect in a regular bail order — surety bonds, restrictions on travel, and sometimes directions not to tamper with evidence or influence witnesses. The quantum of the surety bond should be reasonable and not so onerous as to effectively deny bail.

What happens after default bail is granted?

A common misconception is that default bail means the case is over. It does not. The investigation continues. The police report, when eventually filed, will be taken on record. The trial will proceed. The accused is simply released from custody because the State failed to complete its investigation in time. The prosecution is free to oppose regular bail at a later stage if circumstances change, but it cannot seek cancellation of default bail merely because the chargesheet has now been filed.

Cancellation of default bail is governed by the same principles as cancellation of any bail — supervening circumstances, violation of bail conditions, or interference with the course of justice. The mere completion of investigation is not a ground.

Section 187 BNSS: what has changed from the old law?

Section 187 of the BNSS largely carries forward the scheme of Section 167 CrPC. The fundamental structure — 60 and 90 days, the right to default bail, the requirement that the accused must apply — remains intact. But there are some procedural changes worth noting.

The BNSS places greater emphasis on the use of audio-visual means for remand proceedings. Section 187 permits the Magistrate to authorise detention through electronic means, which has implications for how the remand date is computed and recorded. The provision also tightens the requirements for the police report, aligning it with the broader digital-first approach of the new criminal codes.

Practitioners should also be alive to the fact that the BNSS introduces certain new timelines for specific categories of cases. Where special statutes — such as the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (NDPS Act) or the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, 1967 (UAPA) — prescribe different periods for completion of investigation, those special provisions override the general 60/90-day rule. Under the NDPS Act, for instance, the period is 180 days, and under UAPA it is also extended. These are not governed by Section 187 BNSS but by the respective special statutes.

Practical guidance for accused persons and their families

If you or someone you know is in judicial custody and the investigation appears to be dragging on, here is what you should do:

  • Know the date of first remand. This is the starting point. Get a copy of the remand order from the court record.
  • Identify the offences. Check whether the offences under investigation attract the 60-day or 90-day period. Your advocate should be able to tell you this from the FIR and the remand application.
  • Track the calendar. Count the days carefully. If the 60th or 90th day is approaching and no chargesheet has been filed, be ready to move an application immediately.
  • File the application promptly. Do not wait. The moment the statutory period expires without a police report, file the default bail application before the Magistrate. Even a day's delay can be fatal if the prosecution files the chargesheet in the interim.
  • Ensure the application is on record. It is not enough to hand a written application to the court clerk. Ensure it is presented before the Magistrate and taken on record, so there is no dispute about the date of filing.

The right to default bail is one of the few absolute protections the law affords to an accused person. It exists precisely because the framers of the criminal procedure code — and now the BNSS — recognised that the State's power to deprive a person of liberty must have limits. An investigation cannot be a carte blanche for indefinite incarceration.

But rights that are not exercised are rights that are lost. And in the context of default bail, the margin for error is razor-thin. A competent advocate who is alive to the timelines, who files the application at the right moment, and who insists on the Magistrate's compliance with the statutory mandate — that is the difference between liberty and continued detention.

At our chambers, we advise clients and their families to raise the question of default bail at the earliest opportunity. It is not a substitute for a merits-based bail application — both can and should be pursued in parallel. But where the State has failed in its obligation to complete the investigation within time, the accused should not bear the burden of that failure.
